Uncertainty Principle and Superposition

Think about a series of experiments.

Electron has two propeties : white/black and hard/soft (Denote by W/B & H/S).

We made boxes that can measure such properties. For instance, white electron and black electron will exit the box from different directions. From the direction we know the white/black property of the electron. Denote ‘C-Box’ (color box) for the boxes that can measure W/B property and similarly, ‘H-Box’ (hardness box) for those can measure ‘H/S’ property.

We have mirrors and combinator that put the two directions together, so that we can continue more complicated experiments. Note that they only change the directions.

Below are the experiments from physicists.

First there are some basic and naive experiments.

  1. Electrons —> C-Box —> W and B —> choose W —> C-Box —> W (repeatability of color / hardness)
  2. Electrons —> H-Box —> H and S —> choose H —>C-Box —> W (50%) and B (50%) (lack of correlation of color with hardness)

Then comes the weird experiment.

  1. Electrons —> C-Box —> W and B —> choose W —> H-Box —> H and S —> choose H —> C-Box —> Not (100%) W, but $50\%$ W and $50\%$ B

Apparently, hardness box tampers with color. Therefore experiment 3 tells us color and hardness property cannot be measured simultaneously, which is the uncertainty principle. Though we know it is white at the begining, as long as we measure the hardness, the color property white is not repeatable anymore.

Then we use combinator to dig deeper.

  1. White electrons —> H-Box —> $50\%$ H and $50\%$ S —> combinator —> H-Box —> $50\%$ H and $50\%$ S

  2. White electrons —> H-Box —> 50% H and 50% S —> combinator —> C-Box —> (100%) W

  3. White electrons —> H-Box —> 50% H and 50% S —> put a wall in the Soft’s path —> combinator —> C-Box —> $50\%$ W and $50\%$ B

Experiment 4 tells us combinator does not change the proberties of electrons. Experiment 5 got totally W because the added combination part act as if H/S property doesn’t be measured, hence the W/B property will not be disturbed. Experiment 6 shows a disturbing one from which we can see the uncertainty principle plays a role.

After we understand the principle of the motion of electrons, we consider the being (or state) of it : what is the state of an electron inside the apparatus in the Experiment 5?

We know it is white, so it cannot be hard or soft, otherwise we will get half-half result by the uncertainty principle. It cannot be both hard and soft since different property will go in different direction.In one path the electron cannot have both properties. It cannot be neither since we can get some results.

Therefore, the only condition is the electron has probability of being hard or soft (but we cannot know what exactly is) and such state is called superposition.

Being in a definite state of position means that being in a superposition of having different values for momentum.

  • Bragg scattering. If we send in a beam of electrons to a crystal and watch out the scattering from different angles, we would find the interference effect. Wave Function

Postulate of Wavefunction

An essential postulate: The configuration or state of a quantum object is completely specified by a wavefunction denoted as $\psi(x)$, $\psi\in\mathbb{C}$ .

Classical: $(\vec p,\vec x)\to$ Quantum: $\psi(\vec x)$

Furthermore, $p(x)=|\psi(x)|^2$ determines the probability (density) that an object in the state $\psi(x)$ will be found at position $x$. Note that the dimension of $\psi$ is square root of length since $\displaystyle \int|\psi|^2 \text{d}x=1$.

Operators and the $Schr\ddot{o}dinger \;Equation$

Common operator

For each observable quantity we have an associate operator.

  • Momentum : $\hat p=-i\hbar \partial_x$
  • Position: $\hat x =x$
  • Energy: $\hat E=\displaystyle \frac{\hat p^2}{2m}+V(\hat x)=-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}+V(x)$
