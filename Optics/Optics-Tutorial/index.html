Lasers

Coherence length

An essential part of the optical system is the laser. Interestingly, I sometimes felt strange about the coherence length of my laser.

In my Digital Optical Phase Conjugation (DOPC) system, I used a 532nm pulsed laser (Explore One 532), which has a coherence length of around 8 mm. Compared to low–coherence lasers used in Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) at micrometers coherence length and CW lasers used in multi-mode fiber (MMF) imaging at meters, this is an intermediate coherence length.

Due to this millimeter coherence length, I had to add an optical delay line in my system and had a tough time tuning it. I was wondering why the senior student left me this rugged laser.

Frequency and wavelength have a relation

$$
f=\frac{c}{\lambda}\implies \Delta f\approx\frac{c\Delta\lambda}{\lambda^2},
$$
where $\Delta \lambda$ is the spectral width, $\lambda$ is the central wavelength, and coherence length $L$ can be approximated by
$$
L=\frac{c}{n\Delta f}\approx \frac{\lambda^2}{n\Delta\lambda},
$$
where $n$ is the refractive index of the medium.

For a pulsed laser, what we consider is bandwidth limited, which means the product of the FWHM temporal and spectral width is a constant $p_0$ (for Gaussian pulse, $p_0=0.44$):
$$
\tau\cdot \Delta f=p_0,
$$
where $\tau$ is the pulse width. Therefore, the coherence length of the pulsed laser is
$$
L=\frac{c\tau}{np_0}.
$$
Now I have the answer: I lose the pulsing property if I use a CW laser with a coherence length in meters. For a CW laser, its ‘pulse width’ $\tau$ is unlimited and, therefore, has a narrow bandwidth $\Delta f$, leading to a large $L$. However, as you can see, the large $L$ related to a long $\tau$, which is undesirable in the DOPC system, where a laser pulse needs to be used to enhance the interaction with an ultrasound pulse.

Example

  • For my Explorer One 532 nm laser from Spectra-Physics that has a pulse width around $\tau=10\ ns$ at a 100 KHz repetition frequency, its calculated coherence length in free space is $L\approx 1.36\ m$.

    • [with a question] However, building up a Michelson interferometer, the coherence length I measured was around $1$ cm (length between two traditional points of the interference fringes). This was even shorter at $3$ mm for an effective interaction with the ultrasound.

  • For my Chameleon 920nm femtosecond laser from Coherent, it has pulse width $\tau=300\ fs$, then its coherence length in free space is $L\approx 0.2\ mm$.

Note

For experiments: when using pulsed lasers, the exposure time of a camera should be carefully chosen to ensure enough pulse numbers in one exposure.

Orbital Angular Momentum

What orbital angular momentum (OAM) exactly is has plagued me for a long time.

A mathematical expression: when light has an electric field
$$
\mathbf{E}\sim e^{-im\phi},
$$
then, the light beam has angular momentum $m\hbar$ per photon.

Fundamentally, to understand OAM, we need to know how to derive it: the momentum density of light is $\mathbf{p}=\vec{S}/c^2=\epsilon_0(\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B}),$ and hence, the angular momentum density of light is $\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{p}=\epsilon_0\mathbf{r}\times(\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B}).$ Next, when we substitute the expression of $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ and integrate, we would get a complete expression of angular momentum $\mathbf{J}$ [3].
$$
\begin{align}
\mathbf{J}&=\epsilon_0\int\mathbf{r}\times(\mathbf{E}\times\mathbf{B})\ \text{d}\mathbf{r}\\
&=\mathbf{L}+\mathbf{S}.
\end{align}
$$
As can be seen, OAM $\mathbf{L}$ and spin angular momentum (SAM) $\mathbf{S}$ are components of the total angular momentum.

Though we understood how OAM is derived, it is still hard to catch the meaning visually. After some attempts, I suddenly found $\phi$ is not the general phase in the Cartesian coordinate system but a polar angle in the spherical polar coordinates:

With this graph, I believe you could understand OAM a little deeper. When $m=1$, it corresponds to one rotating phase, since $\phi$ is a polar angle that will grow from $0$ to $2\pi$. When $m=2$ the phase will be wrapped as it grows helically over $2\pi.$

Stokes Parameter

Here, I display some polarization optics concepts. I highly recommend reading Chapter 8 of the Book [5].

Assume the light is quasi-monochromatic with frequency $\bar\omega$, consider the electric field $\mathbf{E}.$ We could factor it into
$$
E_x(t)=a_1(t)e^{i(\phi_1(t)-\bar\omega t)},\\
E_y(t)=a_2(t)e^{i(\phi_2(t)-\bar\omega t)}，
$$
and let $\delta(t)=\phi_1(t)-\phi_2(t).$ The trajectory of $(E_x(t),E_y(t))$ is an ellipse called the polarization ellipse [6]. And the polarization matrix is defined as
$$
\mathbf{J}=
\begin{bmatrix}
\langle E_x^*(t)E_x(t)\rangle & \langle E_x^*(t)E_y(t)\rangle\\
\langle E_y^*(t)E_x(t)\rangle & \langle E_y^*(t)E_y(t)\rangle
\end{bmatrix}=
\begin{bmatrix}
J_{xx}&J_{xy}\\
J_{yx}&J_{yy}
\end{bmatrix}.
$$
It is worth noting that the correlation coefficient
$$
j_{xy}=\frac{J_{xy}}{\sqrt{J_{xx}}\sqrt{J_{yy}}}
$$
determines whether the light is polarized ($|j_{xy}|=1$) / partially polarized / unpolarized ($j_{xy}=0$). Assume $\lambda_1,\lambda_2$ be the eigenvalue of $\mathbf{J}$, then the degree of polarization (DOP) is defined by
$$
\mathcal{P}=\frac{I^{(P)}}{I}=\frac{|\lambda_1-\lambda_2|}{\lambda_1+\lambda_2}.
$$
Now we introduce the Stokes parameter :
$$
\begin{align}
s_0& =\langle a_1^2(t)\rangle+\langle a_2^2(t)\rangle=J_{xx}+J_{yy},\\
s_1&=\langle a_1^2(t)\rangle-\langle a_2^2(t)\rangle=J_{xx}-J_{yy},\\
s_2&=2\langle a_1(t)a_2(t)\cos\delta(t)\rangle=J_{xy}+J_{yx},\\
s_3&=2\langle a_1(t)a_2(t)\sin\delta(t)\rangle=i(J_{yx}-J_{xy}).
\end{align}
$$
We can see clearly that $s_0^2=s_1^2+s_2^2+s_3^2,$ so point $P=[s_1,s_2,s_3]$ is on a sphere which is called the Poincaré sphere. Every point on the sphere represents a state of polarization.

Stokes parameter can also be written in the expression of polarization ellipse parameters:
$$
\begin{align}
s_1&=s_0\cos(2\chi)\cos(2\psi),\\
s_2&=s_0\cos(2\chi)\sin(2\psi),\\
s_3&=s_0\sin(2\chi).
\end{align}
$$
Here the angle $\psi\in[0,\pi]$ is the orientation of the polarization ellipse, $\chi\in[-\pi/2,\pi/2]$ characterizes ellipticity. Right-handed polarized light corresponds to the above-equator sphere, and linearly polarized light lies in the equator plane.

We now conclude: the Stokes parameter describes the polarization state of light; it can mutually convert with the polarization matrix and polarization ellipse.

Holography

Digital blazed grating

Blazed grating is a very useful tool for removing zeroth order (unmodulated) output of the SLM. Now I derive its principle.

Let the period of the grating is $T$ pixels, the maximum modulation phase is $A\in(0,1)$, the complex amplitude of the one-period grating is
$$
t_0(x)=\mathrm{rect}\left(\frac{x-T/2}{T}\right)\cdot \exp\left(i\frac{2\pi A x}{T}\right)
$$
where $\mathrm{rect}$ is defined as
$$
\mathrm{rect}(x)=\Pi(x)=\begin{cases}
0 & \text { if }|x|>=\frac{1}{2} \\
1 & \text { if }|x|<\frac{1}{2}.
\end{cases}
$$
Let the whole grating displayed on SLM have $M$ periods ($M=\lfloor W/T\rfloor$), the whole-period grating is
$$
t(x)=t_0(x)*\sum_{m=-\lceil M/2\rceil+1}^{\lfloor M/2\rfloor}\delta(x-mT).
$$
Note that the sum
$$
\sum_{m=M_1}^{M_2} e^{-i2\pi md}=e^{-i2\pi M_1d}\frac{1-e^{-i2\pi(M_2-M_1+1)d}}{1-e^{-i2\pi d}},
$$
we have the focal plane complex amplitude:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\hat t(u)&=\mathcal{F}\left[t(x)\right]\\
&\approx\left[Te^{-i\pi Tu}\mathrm{sinc}\left(\frac{u}{T}\right)*\delta\left(u-\frac{A}{T}\right)\right]\cdot\sum_{m=-\lceil M/2\rceil+1}^{\lfloor M/2\rfloor}e^{-i2\pi mTu}\\
&=Te^{i\pi Tu}\mathrm{sinc}\left(\frac{u-A/T}{T}\right)\cdot\frac{1-e^{-i2\pi MTu}}{1-e^{-i2\pi Tu}}e^{-i2\pi(-\lceil M/2\rceil+1)Tu}\\
&=Te^{i\pi(2\lceil M/2\rceil-M) Tu}\mathrm{sinc}\left(\frac{u-A/T}{T}\right)\cdot\frac{\sin\left(\pi MTu\right)}{\sin(\pi Tu)}
\end{aligned}
$$
where $\mathrm{sinc(x)}=\sin(\pi x)/(\pi x)$.

Finally, the intensity profile is
$$
I(u)=T^2\mathrm{sinc}^2\left(\frac{u-A/T}{T}\right)\cdot\left(\frac{\sin\left(\pi MTu\right)}{\sin(\pi Tu)}\right)^2
$$
where $\sin^2$ term determines the diffraction order profile, and $\mathrm{sinc}^2$ term dertermines the wrap profile related to the blaze angle. From the fomula we can see that $T$ controls the interval between different diffraction orders. Both $A$ and $T$ affects the diffraction efficiency of the 1st order.

Amplitude modulation with blazed grating

Let $A=A(x)$ and the target amplitude modulation pattern be $a(x)$.

We need
$$
\min_A \left|\mathrm{sinc}\left(\frac{u-A/T}{T}\right)-a\right|_2
$$

Microscopy Basics

Objective Lens

The back pupil size of an objective lens can be calculated by its focal length and NA. The focal length $f$ can be calculated from the objective lens’ magnification:
$$
\mathrm{Mag}=\frac{f_{tubelens}}{f}.
$$
Take a Nikon 40x NA 0.6 objective as an example: the back pupil diameter is
$$
D=2f\cdot \mathrm{NA}=2\cdot f_{tubelens}/\mathrm{Mag}\cdot \mathrm{NA}=6\text{ mm}
$$
which is precisely what we measured.

Here, we should know that the tube lens focal length of the Nikon and Olympus objectives are 200 mm and 180 mm, respectively.

Seeing through Scattering Media

Let the input filed be $E_{in}\in\mathbb{C}^{N}$. It has $N$ modes, where $N=h\times w$ is the input image number. The transmission matrix (TM) that describe the scattering media is $T\in\mathbb{C}^{M\times N}$, and the output field is $E_{out}\in\mathbb{C}^{M}$, $M=H\times W$ is the output image pixels. They have relation:
$$
E_{out}=TE_{in}.
$$
Assume we modulate the $N$ input modes, and $E_{in}=[a_1e^{i\varphi_1},\dots,a_ne^{i\varphi_n}]^T.$ Without modulation, the output field mode $m$ is
$$
E_{m}=\sum_{i=1}^N t_{mn}a_ne^{i\varphi_n}
$$

Peak Ratio and Enhancement

We derive phase and amplitude modulation for focusing through a scattering media. Assume $t_{mn}\in\mathcal{CN}(0,1)$ (a Circular Gaussian), which means both the real and imag part of $t_{mn}$ obey a Gaussian distribution $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Let $\varphi_n=0$, the focusing position be $m^*$, and the modulation term be $\mathbf{b}=[b_1e^{i\phi_1},\dots,b_ne^{i\phi_n}]^T$. Then:
$$
E_{out,peak}=\sum_{n=1}^{N}\left|t_{m^\star n}\right|a_n b_n e^{i[\arg(t_{m^\star n})+\phi_n]}.
$$

  • Uniform input, $I_{in}=|\sum_{i=1}^NE_{in,i}|^2=1\implies a_n=1/\sqrt{N}$
  • For phase modulation: $b_n=1,\phi_n=-\arg(t_{m^*n})$
  • Binary amplitude modulation: $b_m\in{0,1},\phi_n=0$

Now we derive the peak ratio
$$
\mathrm{PR}=I_{p}/I_{in}=|E_{out,peak}|^2
$$
and enhancement factor
$$
\eta=\frac{\mathrm{PR}}{(1-\mathrm{PR})/(N-1)}=\frac{(N-1)\mathrm{PR}}{1-\mathrm{PR}}
$$
for phase and binary amplitude modulation.

Phase modulation

$$
\mathrm{PR}=\left|\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\sum_{i=1}^N|t_{m^\star n}|\right|^2=N\cdot\mathbb{E}^2\left(|t_{m^\star n}|\right)
$$

Here $t_{mn}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2N}}(x+iy),\ x,y\in\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, where $1/\sqrt{2N}$ is for energy conservation. Note
$$
p(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/2},\ p(y)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-y^2/2}.
$$
We have
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{E}(|t_{m^\star n}|)&=\iint_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{1}{\sqrt{2N}}\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\cdot p(x)\cdot p(y)\ \mathrm{d}x\mathrm{d}y \\
&=\frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{2N}}\iint_{-\infty}^\infty \sqrt{x^2+y^2}e^{-(x^2+y^2)/2}\ \mathrm{d}x\mathrm{d}y\\
&=\frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{2N}}\cdot 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}=\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{N}}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore, the peak ratio of phase modulation, theoretically, is $\pi/4.$
